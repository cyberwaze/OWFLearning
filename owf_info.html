Offshore Wind Farm Learning Area

Offshore wind farms (OWF) consist of multiple turbines all connected and communicating with each other. The protocols used to run the turbines within the farm itself travel over wired connections that typically have little to no authentication, encryption or other security features. In other words, they pass around instructions that can be easily intercepted, manipulated or spoofed. There also are wireless communications from the base of the towers to the power-generating components on top.

Underwater and underground cables carry the generated electric power back to shore, passing through an offshore substation, an onshore substation, and ultimately feeding into the grid. Operators at control centers monitor and control the production and flow of power by sending commands to the farm. The farm communicates sensor data back to the center to help operators fine tune performance. This creates a two-way wireless communication path between the farm and the control center.

To help regulate power flow, grid operators communicate with the farm's control center via dedicated communication lines or the public Internet. Enterprise systems may connect to these centers via public Internet connections for receiving data and providing services. All these communication paths can be vulnerable to cybersecurity attacks.

There are confirmed hacks on onshore wind farms attributed to nation-state actors as far back as 2014 and as recently as May 2019. Offshore wind farms rely on the same information and operational technology as onshore wind farms, which means we can expect them to be vulnerable to similar attacks.
